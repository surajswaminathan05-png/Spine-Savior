SPINE SAVIOR: A WEARABLE MULTI-SENSOR BIOFEEDBACK SYSTEM FOR REAL-TIME SPINAL POSTURE MONITORING, ON-DEVICE MACHINE LEARNING CLASSIFICATION, AND HAPTIC CORRECTIVE FEEDBACK


ABSTRACT

Spine Savior is an integrated wearable biofeedback device that continuously monitors the orientation of the human vertebral column across three anatomical spinal regions — cervical (C1 through C7), thoracic (T1 through T12), and lumbar (L1 through L5) — using an array of three 9-degree-of-freedom (9-DOF) inertial measurement unit (IMU) sensors. Sensor fusion data is transmitted wirelessly via Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE) to a real-time browser-based application, which renders a physiologically accurate, biomechanically constrained 3D model of the spine, classifies postural deviations using an on-device 1-Dimensional Convolutional Neural Network (1D-CNN) trained via TensorFlow.js, delivers dynamic coaching through Google Gemini Nano (a local large language model), and activates dual haptic vibration motors mounted on the user's body to deliver immediate tactile corrective alerts when poor posture is detected. The entire system — from sensor to feedback — operates without any cloud dependency, ensuring complete user data privacy.


SECTION 1: PROBLEM STATEMENT AND MOTIVATION

Poor posture is one of the most prevalent ergonomic health risks in modern society. The American Chiropractic Association estimates that approximately 80 percent of the population will experience back pain at some point in their lives, much of it attributable to sustained postural misalignment. Forward head posture (anterior head carriage), thoracic kyphosis (slouching), excessive lumbar lordosis, and lateral spinal tilt are the primary pathological deviations. Existing commercial solutions either use simple single-axis tilt sensors (such as the Upright Go) or camera-based estimation (such as smartphone apps), which lack the multi-regional anatomical precision required for clinically meaningful posture assessment.

Spine Savior addresses this gap by deploying a distributed sensor array that maps the orientation of all three major spinal regions independently, applies clinically-sourced biomechanical range-of-motion (ROM) constraints, and provides multimodal corrective feedback through visual, auditory/textual, and haptic (tactile vibration) channels.


SECTION 2: HARDWARE ARCHITECTURE

2.1 Sensor Array: Three BNO055 9-DOF Absolute Orientation IMUs

The backbone of the sensing system is three Bosch BNO055 inertial measurement units, each containing three independent sensor elements. The first is a triaxial accelerometer (with selectable ranges of plus or minus 2g, 4g, 8g, or 16g), which measures linear acceleration and the static gravitational vector to determine tilt relative to the earth's surface. The second is a triaxial gyroscope (up to plus or minus 2000 degrees per second), which measures angular velocity for tracking rapid rotational movements. The third is a triaxial magnetometer (plus or minus 1300 microtesla on X and Y axes, plus or minus 2500 microtesla on the Z axis), which measures the earth's magnetic field for absolute heading reference.

What distinguishes the BNO055 from cheaper IMUs (such as the MPU-6050) is its onboard ARM Cortex-M0+ microprocessor running a proprietary sensor fusion algorithm. This algorithm combines the raw 9-axis data using an extended Kalman filter to output pre-computed Euler angles (heading, pitch, roll) with absolute orientation in 3D space, eliminating the need for external sensor fusion software and mitigating gyroscope drift over time.

The three sensors are placed on the body as follows. Sensor 1 is placed at the cervical region (the neck), monitoring vertebrae C1 through C7, connected to port 3 of the multiplexer. Sensor 2 is placed at the thoracic region (the upper and mid-back), monitoring vertebrae T1 through T12, connected to port 0 of the multiplexer. Sensor 3 is placed at the lumbar region (the lower back), monitoring vertebrae L1 through L5, connected to port 2 of the multiplexer.

Each sensor is housed in a custom 3D-printed ABS or PLA snap-fit enclosure (designed in OpenSCAD, exported to STL for slicing) and attached to the user's back via an adjustable elastic strap harness.

2.2 I-Squared-C Multiplexer: TCA9548A

All three BNO055 sensors share the same factory-default I-squared-C address (0x28). Because the I-squared-C protocol identifies devices by address, placing three identically-addressed devices on the same bus would cause an address collision. The Texas Instruments TCA9548A 8-channel I-squared-C multiplexer resolves this. It sits at address 0x70 and acts as a channel switch, electrically connecting the microcontroller's I-squared-C bus to only one downstream channel at a time. The firmware cycles through three channels (ports 0, 2, and 3) in rapid succession, reading all three sensors sequentially within each polling cycle.

2.3 Microcontroller: Arduino Nano 33 BLE

The Arduino Nano 33 BLE is the system's embedded controller. It is built around the Nordic Semiconductor nRF52840 system-on-chip (SoC), which contains an ARM Cortex-M4F processor at 64 MHz (with a hardware floating-point unit for fast Euler angle processing), an integrated Bluetooth Low Energy 5.0 radio (2.4 GHz ISM band) enabling direct wireless communication with any BLE-capable host without an external Bluetooth module, 256 KB of RAM, and 1 MB of flash memory.

The firmware operates as follows. During initialization, the firmware initializes the I-squared-C bus, configures each BNO055 via the TCA9548A multiplexer (calling a channel-select function then beginning communication for ports 0, 2, and 3), and enables the external 32.768 kHz crystal oscillator on each sensor for higher accuracy fusion. Then, a custom BLE GATT (Generic Attribute Profile) service is registered with a 128-bit UUID. A single BLE characteristic within this service is configured as readable and notifiable with a 36-byte payload (9 floats multiplied by 4 bytes each). The device advertises itself as "SpineSavior" and is discoverable by any compatible BLE scanner or browser supporting the Web Bluetooth API.

In the main loop, which runs at approximately 60 Hz, when a BLE central device (the browser) connects, the firmware enters a tight polling loop. For each of the three sensors, it selects the multiplexer channel then retrieves the latest Euler angles (heading, pitch, roll). The nine float values are packed into a C struct with packed attribute to prevent compiler-inserted padding, ensuring an exact 36-byte binary payload. The payload is written to the BLE characteristic. A 16-millisecond delay enforces the approximately 60 Hz data rate.

2.4 Haptic Feedback Actuators: Dual DC Vibration Motors

Two 10-millimeter by 3-millimeter flat coin-type DC vibration motors (ERM, meaning Eccentric Rotating Mass motors) are integrated into the wearable harness. These motors operate at 3 volts DC and spin an off-center mass at approximately 12,000 RPM, generating a perceptible tactile vibration similar to a smartphone vibration alert. The motors are made of stainless steel and measure 0.39 inches wide by 0.12 inches tall.

The two motors are positioned on the user's body at locations corresponding to the cervical and lumbar regions. When the machine learning classifier detects any postural deviation (forward head, slouching, lordosis, or lateral tilt), the system signals the Arduino to activate the corresponding vibration motor or motors, providing an immediate somatosensory cue to the user. This haptic modality is critical because it allows posture correction even when the user is not looking at the screen — during studying, reading, or working — delivering a tactile "nudge" that operates below conscious awareness threshold yet triggers corrective postural adjustment.

Each motor is driven through a standard NPN transistor switching circuit. An Arduino GPIO pin (3.3-volt logic high) drives the base of an NPN transistor (such as a 2N2222A) through a 1-kilohm base resistor. The transistor's collector is connected to the motor, and the emitter is connected to ground. A flyback diode (1N4001) is wired reverse-parallel across the motor terminals to suppress back-EMF voltage spikes when the motor is de-energized, protecting the transistor and microcontroller from inductive kickback. Motor power is sourced from the 3.3-volt or battery voltage rail of the Arduino.

The vibration pattern is designed for maximum perceptibility without being disruptive. A short double-pulse pattern (200 milliseconds on, 100 milliseconds off, 200 milliseconds on) is used for moderate deviations. Continuous vibration of up to 2 seconds is used for severe deviations exceeding 75 percent of the ROM threshold. A minimum 30-second cooldown period between haptic alerts prevents habituation (sensory adaptation), where the user's nervous system would begin to ignore a constant stimulus.

2.5 3D-Printed Enclosures

All electronic modules are housed in custom-designed snap-fit enclosures modeled in OpenSCAD (a parametric, script-based CAD tool) and exported as STL files for 3D printing via FDM (Fused Deposition Modeling). The central hub enclosure measures 3.5 inches by 2.0 inches by 0.5 inches and houses the Arduino Nano 33 BLE, TCA9548A multiplexer, LiPo battery, vibration motor driver circuitry, and wiring harness connectors. The sensor enclosures come in two form factors (1.4 inches by 1.5 inches and 2.0 inches by 1.5 inches, both 0.5 inches tall) and house individual BNO055 breakout boards with routed I-squared-C cable channels. Design features include rounded corners with a 2-millimeter fillet radius, snap-fit lid closures requiring no screws, ventilation slots for passive thermal management, and cable routing channels.

2.6 Power Supply

The entire wearable system is powered by a 3.7-volt lithium-polymer (LiPo) battery connected to the Arduino Nano 33 BLE's VIN pin, providing portable operation. The BNO055 sensors draw approximately 12 milliamps each (36 milliamps total), the Arduino draws approximately 50 milliamps, and the vibration motors draw approximately 80 milliamps each when active, yielding a total peak system current of approximately 250 milliamps (with both motors running), enabling several hours of continuous use on a standard 500 to 1000 milliamp-hour cell.


SECTION 3: COMMUNICATION PROTOCOL — BLUETOOTH LOW ENERGY (BLE)

The wireless link between the wearable hardware and the software application uses Bluetooth Low Energy 5.0 (also known as Bluetooth Smart), a low-power wireless protocol optimized for intermittent, small-packet data transfer. The physical layer operates on the 2.4 GHz ISM band at a 1 Mbps data rate (BLE 5.0 also supports 2 Mbps LE 2M PHY). The protocol stack uses GATT (Generic Attribute Profile) over ATT (Attribute Protocol) over L2CAP. The connection interval is negotiated between the central device (the browser) and the peripheral device (the Arduino); the firmware writes data every 16 milliseconds. The payload is a single 36-byte binary blob containing 9 IEEE 754 single-precision floating-point values in little-endian byte order, matching the ARM Cortex-M4's native format.

On the software side, the Web Bluetooth API (supported in Chromium-based browsers including Chrome 89 and later, and Edge) enables the web application to directly discover, connect to, and subscribe to BLE GATT notifications from the Arduino — entirely in JavaScript, with no native drivers, platform SDKs, or server middleware required.


SECTION 4: SOFTWARE ARCHITECTURE

The entire front-end application is a single-page web application (SPA) built with vanilla HTML5, CSS3, and ES6+ JavaScript modules. There is no build system, no npm dependencies, and no server-side logic. The application loads instantly from a static file server.

4.1 Real-Time 3D Spinal Visualization Using Three.js

The application renders a high-fidelity, anatomically accurate 3D model of the human vertebral column using Three.js (version r160), a WebGL-based 3D graphics library. The model is in GLB (Binary glTF) format, sourced from anatomical repositories, containing individually segmented meshes for 5 lumbar vertebrae (L1 through L5), 12 thoracic vertebrae (T1 through T12), 7 cervical vertebrae (C1 through C7), intervertebral discs (cosmetic, non-articulating), and the sacrum (fixed, non-movable).

The integration pipeline works as follows. First, the GLB is parsed by the GLTFLoader. Each mesh is identified, and its world-space bounding box center is computed. Second, meshes are sorted by vertical Y-position from inferior to superior. Third, intervertebral discs (identified by a specific material) are separated from vertebral bodies, and the sacrum is excluded from articulation. Fourth, each movable vertebra is wrapped in a Three.js Group pivot node positioned at the mesh's centroid, enabling rotation about its anatomical center of rotation.

Rendering features include ACES Filmic tone mapping for cinematic lighting, three-point studio lighting (key light, fill light, and rim light) with PCF soft shadow mapping using a 1024-by-1024 shadow map, hemisphere light for ambient environmental fill, OrbitControls for interactive camera orbit, pan, and zoom, and a 60 frames-per-second render loop via requestAnimationFrame.

4.2 Biomechanical Motion Model

Translating three discrete sensor readings into fluid, biologically plausible motion across 24 individually articulating vertebrae requires three layers of biomechanical computation.

4.2.1 Gaussian Falloff Blending

Each sensor's influence on nearby vertebrae is distributed using a Gaussian (normal distribution) weighting function. The weight for each vertebra is calculated as the exponential of the negative of the square of the distance between the vertebra's global index and the sensor's global index, divided by two times the square of sigma. The global indices are as follows: lumbar sensor is at index 2, thoracic sensor at index 12, and cervical sensor at index 20. Sigma is set to 4.0 (the blend width parameter, empirically tuned).

This creates a smooth bell-shaped influence curve: vertebrae directly at the sensor position receive full weight, while distant vertebrae receive exponentially decaying influence. The total contribution from all three sensors is normalized to sum to 1.0, preventing double-counting.

4.2.2 Per-Vertebra Flexibility Weighting

Not all vertebrae in a spinal region contribute equally to regional motion. The flexibility weights are derived from PubMed-published in-vivo segmental range-of-motion studies. In the lumbar region, L4-L5 and L5-S1 contribute the most flexion and extension at 24 percent each, while L1-L2 contributes the least at 16 percent. In the thoracic region, motion is relatively evenly distributed at 6 to 10 percent per level, reflecting rib cage constraint. In the cervical region, C1-C2 (the atlas-axis joint) accounts for the majority of axial rotation at 25 percent each, while lower cervical segments (C3 through C7) contribute more evenly at 10 percent each.

Each sensor's contribution is scaled by the target vertebra's flexibility weight and the number of vertebrae in its region, accurately simulating how spinal motion is distributed in real human anatomy.

4.2.3 Range-of-Motion (ROM) Clamping

Every intervertebral joint has hard anatomical limits on how far it can move, which vary dramatically by region and axis. For example, the C1-C2 level (the atlas-axis joint) permits 15.0 degrees of flexion/extension, 8.0 degrees of lateral bending, and 40.0 degrees of axial rotation, enabling you to turn your head. The T6-T7 level permits only 2.5 degrees of flexion/extension, 3.5 degrees of lateral bending, and 4.0 degrees of axial rotation, due to rib cage restrictions. The L4-L5 level permits 14.3 degrees of flexion/extension, 8.8 degrees of lateral bending, and only 2.1 degrees of axial rotation, since the lower back barely twists.

The full ROM limits used in the system are as follows. In the lumbar region: L1-L2 has limits of 9.5, 8.4, and 1.6 degrees; L2-L3 has 10.1, 9.8, and 2.4 degrees; L3-L4 has 11.2, 12.0, and 1.3 degrees; L4-L5 has 14.3, 8.8, and 2.1 degrees; and L5-S1 has 14.3, 7.6, and 1.6 degrees (all in flexion/extension, lateral bending, and axial rotation respectively). In the thoracic region, the values per level from T1-T2 down to T12-L1 are: 4.0/6.0/9.0, 4.0/6.0/8.0, 4.0/5.0/7.0, 3.5/5.0/6.0, 3.0/4.0/5.0, 2.5/3.5/4.0, 2.5/3.5/4.0, 3.0/4.0/5.0, 3.5/5.0/5.0, 4.0/5.0/4.0, 5.0/5.0/3.0, and 5.0/5.0/3.0 degrees. In the cervical region: C1-C2 has 15.0/8.0/40.0, C2-C3 has 15.0/8.0/40.0, C3-C4 has 10.0/10.0/7.0, C4-C5 has 10.0/10.0/7.0, C5-C6 has 10.0/10.0/7.0, C6-C7 has 8.0/7.0/5.0, and C7-T1 has 5.0/4.0/3.0 degrees.

These limits are sourced from clinically published segmental ROM data. The software clamps all computed rotations to these limits, preventing the 3D model from ever bending in biologically impossible ways.

The global regional ROM totals used for posture scoring normalization are: cervical has 64 degrees flexion/extension, 49 degrees lateral bending, and 85 degrees axial rotation; thoracic has 26 degrees flexion/extension, 30 degrees lateral bending, and 47 degrees axial rotation; and lumbar has 65 degrees flexion/extension, 30 degrees lateral bending, and 15.3 degrees axial rotation.

4.2.4 Critically Damped Spring-Damper Smoothing

Raw IMU data is inherently noisy due to sensor jitter, ADC quantization noise, and magnetic interference. To produce smooth, natural-looking motion, each vertebra's rotation is filtered through a critically damped second-order spring-damper system. The algorithm works as follows: the acceleration equals the stiffness constant K multiplied by the difference between the target rotation and the current rotation, minus the damping coefficient D multiplied by the current velocity. The velocity is then updated by adding the acceleration multiplied by the time step. The position (rotation) is then updated by adding the new velocity multiplied by the time step.

The spring stiffness constant K is set to 150.0. The damping coefficient D is set to 2 times the square root of K, which equals approximately 24.5. This satisfies the critical damping ratio (zeta equals 1.0). The time step delta-t is 1/60th of a second (one frame at 60 frames per second).

Critical damping is the mathematical sweet spot: the system converges to the target rotation as fast as possible without ever overshooting. This is the same dynamic model used for automotive suspension systems, camera gimbal stabilization, and AAA game character animation. The result is a 3D spine that moves fluidly and heavily, like real tissue and bone, rather than snapping jerkily to each new sensor reading.

4.3 Posture Scoring Algorithm

The system continuously computes a posture score from 0 to 100 based on the aggregate deviation from the calibrated baseline (ideal posture). For each region (cervical, thoracic, lumbar), the absolute difference between the current sensor angles and the calibrated baseline is computed. Each deviation is normalized by the region's total ROM (for example, 64 degrees of flexion/extension for cervical, 26 degrees for thoracic, 65 degrees for lumbar), converting absolute degrees into a 0.0 to 1.0 fraction of maximum possible deviation. The six normalized deviations (pitch plus roll for each of three regions) are averaged. The score is calculated as the maximum of 0 or the rounded value of 100 times the quantity (1 minus 2 times the average deviation).

This ROM-normalized approach ensures that a 10-degree deviation in the thoracic spine (which has only 26 degrees total ROM) is correctly weighted as more severe than a 10-degree deviation in the cervical spine (which has 64 degrees total ROM).

4.4 Clinical Alert System

The application generates real-time clinical-style alerts using ROM-relative thresholds. The warning level is triggered when deviation exceeds 50 percent of regional ROM (for example, greater than 32 degrees of cervical pitch shift). The severe level is triggered when deviation exceeds 75 percent of regional ROM.

Alert categories include forward head posture (cervical pitch deviation), head lateral tilt (cervical roll deviation), thoracic slouching (thoracic pitch deviation), lumbar over-flexion (lumbar pitch deviation), and lumbar over-rotation (lumbar heading deviation).


SECTION 5: MACHINE LEARNING — ON-DEVICE POSTURE CLASSIFICATION

5.1 Framework: TensorFlow.js

The ML system uses TensorFlow.js version 4.17, Google's JavaScript implementation of the TensorFlow deep learning framework. All model training and inference execute directly in the browser's main thread via WebGL-accelerated tensor operations. No training data or model weights ever leave the user's device.

5.2 Data Collection: Guided Training Wizard

The application includes a guided training wizard that walks the user through recording five distinct postural classes. Class 1 is Normal, meaning upright, neutral spine alignment. Class 2 is Forward Head (anterior head carriage), meaning the chin protrudes forward of the shoulder line. Class 3 is Slouching (thoracic kyphosis), meaning excessive rounding of the upper back. Class 4 is Lordosis, meaning excessive inward curvature of the lower back. Class 5 is Lateral Tilt, meaning asymmetric lean to one side.

For each posture, the user holds the position for 30 to 90 seconds. At 60 Hz, this yields 1,800 to 5,400 frames per class. Each frame is a 9-dimensional feature vector containing the heading, pitch, and roll for the cervical sensor, then the heading, pitch, and roll for the thoracic sensor, then the heading, pitch, and roll for the lumbar sensor.

5.3 Preprocessing: Sliding Window Segmentation

Raw frames are segmented into overlapping sliding windows of 60 frames (1 second of data at 60 Hz), with a 50 percent overlap (step size of 30 frames). Each window becomes a training sample with shape 60 by 9, representing a 60-timestep sequence of 9-channel sensor data. This temporal context is critical because it allows the model to learn the temporal dynamics of each posture, not just static snapshots.

5.4 Model Architecture: 1D Convolutional Neural Network (1D-CNN)

The neural network architecture is a 1D-CNN (1-Dimensional Convolutional Neural Network), specifically designed for time-series classification. The architecture is as follows.

The first layer is a 1D convolutional layer with an input shape of 60 timesteps by 9 channels, 32 filters, a kernel size of 5, ReLU activation, and "same" padding. This produces an output of shape 60 by 32 with 1,472 parameters. The second layer is a max pooling layer with pool size 2, reducing the output to shape 30 by 32. The third layer is another 1D convolutional layer with 64 filters, a kernel size of 3, ReLU activation, and "same" padding, producing an output of shape 30 by 64 with 6,208 parameters. The fourth layer is another max pooling layer with pool size 2, reducing the output to shape 15 by 64. The fifth layer is a flatten layer, converting the output to a 1-dimensional vector of 960 elements. The sixth layer is a dense (fully connected) layer with 64 units and ReLU activation, with 61,504 parameters. The seventh layer is a dropout layer with a 30 percent dropout rate for regularization. The eighth and final layer is a dense layer with 5 units and softmax activation, outputting a 5-class probability distribution, with 325 parameters. The total number of trainable parameters is approximately 69,509.

The reason we chose a 1D-CNN is that, unlike fully connected networks (which would treat each timestep independently), convolutional layers slide learned filters across the time axis, detecting local temporal patterns such as the characteristic gradual drift of forward head posture versus the sudden drop of slouching. Max pooling layers downsample the temporal resolution, creating a hierarchical feature representation. This architecture is standard in inertial sensor-based human activity recognition (HAR) literature.

5.5 Training Configuration

The optimizer is Adam with a learning rate of 0.001, an adaptive gradient descent algorithm. The loss function is categorical cross-entropy, the standard for multi-class classification. Training runs for 20 epochs (full passes over the training set). The batch size is 32 samples per gradient update. A 20 percent validation split holds out data for overfitting detection. A 30 percent dropout rate before the final layer prevents overfitting to the small per-user dataset. Trained model weights are serialized to localStorage using TensorFlow.js's built-in model saving, allowing the model to persist across browser sessions without requiring the user to retrain.

5.6 Live Inference Pipeline

During real-time operation, every frame, the 9-channel sensor vector is pushed into a rolling buffer of length 60. Every 60 frames (approximately 1 second), the buffer is converted to a tensor of shape 1 by 60 by 9 and passed through the model's predict function. The softmax output yields a probability distribution over the 5 classes. The class with the highest probability is selected as the prediction, along with its confidence score. The predicted label drives both the AI coaching system and the haptic feedback actuators.


SECTION 6: ARTIFICIAL INTELLIGENCE — ON-DEVICE COACHING VIA GOOGLE GEMINI NANO

6.1 Google Gemini Nano: Chrome's Built-In LLM

When the ML classifier identifies a postural deviation, the system generates a personalized, context-aware coaching message using Google Gemini Nano, a compact large language model (LLM) embedded directly in Chrome's rendering engine via the window.ai.languageModel API.

Key characteristics are as follows. All language model computation runs locally on the user's CPU or GPU, so no data is sent to any server. The model is initialized with a system prompt that reads: "You are a brief, friendly posture coach for a student. Always respond with exactly 1 to 2 sentences of encouraging, actionable advice. Never use medical jargon." Each prompt includes the specific ML classification label (for example, "Forward Head") and the real-time Euler angle readings (for example, "Cervical pitch: 22.4 degrees, Thoracic pitch: negative 8.1 degrees"), enabling the LLM to generate dynamically tailored, data-aware responses rather than generic advice.

6.2 Expert System Fallback

If Gemini Nano is not available on the user's device (for example, due to an unsupported browser version or hardware limitations), the system seamlessly falls back to a deterministic expert rule-based system containing 15 curated coaching tips (3 per posture class), authored based on physical therapy and ergonomic best practices. Examples include: "Try tucking your chin slightly — imagine a string pulling the top of your head up" for forward head posture, "You're rounding your upper back. Try squeezing your shoulder blades together" for slouching, and "Your lower back is arching too much. Engage your core to flatten it slightly" for lordosis. The tips rotate cyclically to avoid repetition. A minimum 30-second interval between coaching updates prevents message fatigue.


SECTION 7: HAPTIC FEEDBACK SUBSYSTEM — TACTILE CORRECTIVE ALERTS

The haptic feedback subsystem represents the system's somatosensory output modality — the only feedback channel that does not require the user to be visually attending to any screen. When the 1D-CNN classifier detects a posture classified as anything other than "normal" with sufficiently high confidence, the classification result is sent back to the Arduino via a BLE write characteristic. The Arduino then activates one or both of the 3-volt DC vibration motors using GPIO-driven NPN transistor switching circuits. The motors generate a perceptible, localized vibration at the relevant anatomical region (cervical or lumbar), alerting the user to correct their posture. This closes a complete sensory-motor feedback loop: the sensors detect the deviation, the neural network classifies it, and the haptic motors physically prompt the user to correct it, all within approximately 1 second of the deviation occurring.


SECTION 8: SYSTEM INTEGRATION AND DATA FLOW

The complete data flow of the system operates as follows. On the wearable hardware, three BNO055 sensors (cervical, thoracic, and lumbar) each produce heading, pitch, and roll angles. These connect via I-squared-C to the TCA9548A multiplexer, which connects via I-squared-C to the Arduino Nano 33 BLE (nRF52840). The Arduino also controls two vibration motors (one for the cervical region and one for the lumbar region) through transistor driver circuits.

The Arduino transmits 36-byte binary packets at 60 Hz wirelessly via Bluetooth Low Energy 5.0 at 2.4 GHz to the browser application. On the browser side, the Web Bluetooth API receives the data and feeds it to the Three.js engine, which renders the 24-bone spine model with ROM constraints and spring-damper smoothing. Simultaneously, the data feeds into the TensorFlow.js 1D-CNN classifier, which outputs a posture classification. That classification drives both the Gemini Nano AI coach (which generates coaching text) and the haptic motor activation commands (which are sent back to the Arduino over BLE).

The browser application outputs include the real-time 3D spinal visualization, a posture score from 0 to 100, clinical alerts, ML classification labels with confidence percentages, AI coaching tips, and haptic motor activation commands.


SECTION 9: KEY TECHNICAL INNOVATIONS

Innovation 1: Multi-regional distributed IMU sensing. Unlike single-sensor commercial devices, Spine Savior independently tracks all three spinal regions, enabling differential diagnosis between cervical, thoracic, and lumbar deviations.

Innovation 2: Clinically-sourced biomechanical constraints. PubMed-published segmental ROM data prevents the 3D model from producing physiologically impossible poses, adding clinical credibility.

Innovation 3: Personalized on-device machine learning. The 1D-CNN is trained on each individual user's body geometry and postural habits, producing a classifier customized to their unique anatomy rather than a generic population model.

Innovation 4: Zero-cloud architecture. Every computation — BLE communication, 3D rendering, neural network training and inference, LLM coaching, and haptic feedback triggering — executes entirely on the user's local hardware, ensuring complete biometric data privacy under HIPAA-aligned design principles.

Innovation 5: Multimodal feedback loop. The system closes the sensory-motor feedback loop through three parallel channels — visual (3D spine model, posture score, and clinical alerts), cognitive (AI coaching text), and somatosensory (haptic vibration) — maximizing the probability of corrective action regardless of the user's attentional state.


SECTION 10: TECHNOLOGIES AND TOOLS

The IMU sensors are three Bosch BNO055 units providing 9-DOF absolute orientation sensing. The multiplexer is a Texas Instruments TCA9548A for I-squared-C address conflict resolution. The microcontroller is an Arduino Nano 33 BLE (Nordic nRF52840) for sensor polling, BLE transmission, and motor control. The haptic actuators are two 10-millimeter by 3-millimeter coin vibration motors for tactile posture correction alerts. The wireless protocol is Bluetooth Low Energy 5.0 for wearable-to-browser communication. The browser API is the Web Bluetooth API for JavaScript-level BLE access. The 3D rendering engine is Three.js version r160 (WebGL) for real-time spinal visualization. The 3D model format is GLB (Binary glTF) for the anatomical spine mesh. The machine learning framework is TensorFlow.js version 4.17 for the 1D-CNN posture classifier. The AI and LLM component is Google Gemini Nano for on-device natural language coaching. The CAD tool is OpenSCAD for parametric enclosure design. The fabrication method is FDM 3D printing for custom hardware housings. The programming languages are C++ (Arduino firmware), JavaScript (ES6+ web application), HTML5, and CSS3.
